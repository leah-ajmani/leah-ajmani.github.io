Emergent Public Health Topics on Wikipedia

Leah Ajmani · Cornell University · MEng Project · lha37@cornell.edu

Background

“Imagine a world in which every single person on the planet is given free access to the sum of all human knowledge. That's what we're doing.” - Jimmy Wales, Founder of Wikipedia

Just a few years after it was founded, Wikipedia was thought to be a fundamental change to the way information is spread globally. Tapscott and Williams [1] founded the theory of Wikinomics: the idea that Wikipedia is a global and ubiquitous platform. However, Wikipedia’s information was deemed questionable. Even popular TV shows, such as The Office, poked fun at the misinformation on Wikipedia. Even researchers have been divided on the topic. In 2010, Bates & Logan called for society to finally underpin Wikipedia as a proper citation source [2]. In 2008, Rask found startling differences in the reach that various Wikipedia language editions have, claiming that “Wikipedia is only for the rich.” In 2015, Fetahu et al. found that Wikipedia information lags behind news sources and, therefore, may have information inconsistencies. In a time where the whole world needs quick and reliable information the most, I thought it would be interesting to revisit whether Wikipedia really is a global and ubiquitous platform.

Methodology

Measures

I specifically wanted to measure the editing patterns on Wikipedia articles related to COVID-19, as edit quantity can indicate community engagement and information reliability. Specifically, I wanted to look at the speed and quantity of edits across different language editions after impactful events, such as WHO announcements and local lockdown orders.

Country Selection

In order to get diversity in human development index, Wikipedia size and location I chose the following countries: English, Italian, French, Chinese, Danish, German, Hungarian, Japanese, Malay, Polish, Russian, Thai, Turkish, Swahili, Bengali, Indonesian. As the map shows, the countries mainly span Europe and Asia. This is because comparison of Wikipedia edits to country-wide coronavirus events, such as cases and deaths, requires a one-to-one mapping of countries to languages. Thus, continents such as South America and Australia were not good candidates for this experiment. While I tried to mostly choose single language countries, exceptions were made for the United States (English) for relevance and Tanzania (Swahili) for diversity in human development index.

Data

All data can be found here in two main files. timeseries.csv contains language, country, new_cases, new_edits, label indexed by date. New cases is the number of new coronavirus cases in the country specified. Case data was taken from here. New edits is the number of new edits on the Wikipedia article for the COVID-19 Pandemic in the specified language. Label labels a date as either a Global Event (such as a WHO announcement), First Death, First Case or Lockdown. All of the labels were hand-coded using various news articles as sources.

wiki-summary-stats.csv contains country, language, wp_code, pagename, link, hdi, length, citation_count, date_created, total_edits and was used for the purposes of accessing the edit histories and ensuring I had obtained diversity within human development indices and article sizes.

Results

Overall Results

Overall, most countries had editting trends that pre-empted case trends or global announcements. However, it's hard to tease out exactly what's going on without zooming in a bit.

Response to Global Events

On Jan 31, 2020 the WHO declared a global emergency due to the spread of COVID-19. I chose to focus on this particular global event because it occured before the virus was severely growing in many nations. As this graph shows, there were disparate responses to this announcement across the selected language editions.

Immediate Responses

These countries had an upward trend in edits leading up to and on the day of the WHO announcement, implying that they were impacted by the global event. Note that these are all high developed countries.

No Responses

These countries had no editting pattern related to the announcement. Note that these are all low developed countries.

Interesting Cases

China and Russia are interesting cases because Wikipedia is not accessible within those two countries. The Swahili language edition did not create a COVID-19 article until about a week after the announcement. On the English language Wikipedia we see edits plateau because discussion moved to a "talk" page to discuss the announcement.

Responses to Local Events

Most countries had an upward trend, or at least a plateau of a downward trend after their lockdown orderes were put in place. The exceptions here being China and Russia, which are not available in their local language and Thailand, which doesn't allow any controversial discussion about the King or his orders on the internet. Note that Tanzania was never put under a lockdown, so the pink dot is the first case in Tanzania.

Discussion

Wikipedia is clearly a global platform, countries ranging from no. 4 to no. 159 on the human development index all have articles about the same emergent health topic, but these results show that it is not a ubiquitous platform. There is a sizable disparity in the way different countries react to WHO announcements, and it seems very dependent on development level. These results demonstrate that Wikipedia should be viewed as an amplifier rather than an informational Garden of Eden. When users are given the resources, such as the accessibility to Wikipedia itself, Wikipedia can be a useful resource that doesn’t trail too far behind the news. However, it can also be another platform for censorship and tech inaccessibility to permeate through.

Most importantly, these results are proof that we are isolated right now, but we are not alone. Despite the pandemic, there are still massive amounts of Wikipedia users having discussions and trying to spread information out there. Communities can exist even in isolation.
